What are deepfakes - and how can you spot them? (EP 1)

Deepfakes are a tool to make videos that resemble legitimate events but are edited, similar to photoshop. The primary use-case so far has been in pornography, but they can also be used to make videos of anyone - including influential figures like politicians and celebrities - where they can be portrayed saying damaging things. The visual and audio components of these videos can be faked using a form of artificial intelligence called deep learning. However, this technology isn’t limited to videos, photos and audio can also be faked.

Sample, Ian. “What Are Deepfakes – and How Can You Spot Them?” The Guardian, Guardian News and Media, 13 Jan. 2020, https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2020/jan/13/what-are-deepfakes-and-how- canyou-spot-them.

Push to rein in social media sweeps the states (EP 2)

Deepfakes: A Digital Transformation Leads to Misinformation (EP 3)

This paper focuses on both deepfakes and the presence of grey media. The paper focuses on video deepfakes present in media. Deepfakes are becoming easier to develop, to the point where an unskilled individual can distribute misinformation without much effort or technological knowledge. In addition, deepfakes are becoming harder to detect and distinguish from real speech. Individuals who already spread misinformation use deepfakes as an advanced means of doing so.

Nour N, Gelfand J. Deepfakes: A Digital Transformation Leads to Misinformation. Conference Papers: International Conference on Grey Literature. 2022;23:55-65. Accessed January 18, 2023. https://search.ebscohost.com/login.aspx?direct=true&db=a9h&AN=160924543&site=eh ost-live

Man behind viral Tom Cruise deepfake videos calls the technology ‘morally neutral’ (EP 1)

This article highlights the ethical issues presented with making deepfake videos containing celebrities. Miles Fisher used deepfake to create videos that make him look like the actor Tom Cruise and posted them on TikTok where they went viral. The article poses the question as to what the intellectual property rights were for the videos, would it belong to Tom Cruise, or to the video’s maker. In addition, concerns are raised on the negative potential use cases of the deepfake technology.

Stump, Scott. “Man behind Viral Tom Cruise Deepfake Videos Calls the Technology 'Morally Neutral'.” TODAY, TODAY, 28 Dec. 2021, https://www.today.com/news/man- tom-cruise-deepfakes-tiktok-speaks-ethics-technology-rcna10163.

Medical Deepfakes Are the Real Deal (EP 1)

This article gives insight into the use of deepfakes in the medical industry. More and more companies developing medical imaging artificial intelligence are being cleared by the FDA for use in the industry. Deepfake algorithms can be used in many use case scenarios within the industry. For example, they can be used to extend x-ray scans to show parts of the body outside the field of view of the scan, allowing doctors and patients to get the full region required to make a diagnosis.

Goth, Greg. “Medical Deepfakes Are the Real Deal.” MDDI, 4 Oct. 2022, https://www.mddionline.com/artificial-intelligence/medical-deepfakes-are-real-deal.

Anticipating and addressing the ethical implications of deepfakes in the context of elections (EP 2, EP 3)

This peer reviewed article highlights the ethical implications of deepfakes, specifically considering its potential impact on elections. Deepfakes can distort the information voters use to make decisions, which can undermine the integrity of the election. The paper outlines possible scenarios where deepfakes could be used in elections: to harm viewers/listeners, harm subjects and harm social institutions. Some of the specific use cases include deception, intimidation, misattribution, and undermining trust.

Diakopoulos, Nicholas, and Deborah Johnson. “Anticipating and Addressing the Ethical Implications of Deepfakes in the Context of Elections.” New Media & Society, vol. 23, no. 7, 2020, pp. 2072–2098., https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444820925811.
